I am a Postdoctoral Associate at **Yale University**, where I work with Prof. Tianyu Zhu. I develop scalable computational methods and software for quantum many-body physics, with a particular focus on strongly correlated electrons. My work brings together quantum chemistry, many-body theory, scientific machine learning, and high-performance computing.

My current research spans scalable quantum many-body methods and AI for quantum materials. I lead the development of [**PyTC**](/projects/pytc/), a JAX-based framework for transcorrelated calculations.

## Current research

- **Low-rank transcorrelated Hamiltonians.** I am developing interpolative separable density fitting (ISDF) for explicitly transcorrelated Hamiltonians, with validation on hydrogen chains and molecular systems including ethylene and benzene.
- **AI for magnetic materials.** I am developing **AI4ARPES**, a physics-informed workflow that combines simulated and measured photoemission spectra to identify magnetic phases. The project uses Fe<sub>1/3</sub>NbS<sub>2</sub> as a benchmark and focuses on closing the simulation-to-experiment gap.
- **Learned wavefunctions and embedding.** I combine transcorrelated Hamiltonians with neural quantum states and quantum-embedding methods to study strongly correlated molecules and materials.

Before joining Yale, I held postdoctoral research positions at **Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich** and the **California Institute of Technology**, and worked at the **Max Planck Institute for Solid State Research**. I received my PhD in quantum chemistry through the International Max Planck Research School for Condensed Matter Science, working with Prof. Ali Alavi and Prof. Andreas Grüneis. I also served as an external scientific consultant at **ByteDance**, advising on machine-learning research for quantum chemistry.

I am open to **research and applied-science roles in AI for science, ML research engineering, and scientific machine learning**. I am also interested in collaborations at the intersection of electronic-structure theory, machine learning, and scientific computing, especially where new algorithms and modern accelerator hardware can make accurate many-body calculations more practical.
